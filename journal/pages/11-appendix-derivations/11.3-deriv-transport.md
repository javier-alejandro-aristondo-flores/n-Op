# Group C — Transport, Thermodynamic, Chemical/Surface

> **Conventions.** κ appears here in both Green–Kubo (total) and electronic (Kubo second-moment) forms; the observable-level decomposition is `κ_total = κ_el + κ_ph` (arch-10). Regimes are referred to by name (the canonical nine-regime table is arch-05's).

Deep mathematical research for the n-Op `/physics` library. Scope: three
regimes that share statistical-mechanical and non-equilibrium structure
(distribution functions, free energies, rate equations). These regimes are
about collective, emergent, statistical behavior built on top of the
microscopic structural and electronic substrate.

The cross-regime synthesis at the end argues for a single shared canonical
form — the GENERIC two-bracket structure with a free-energy / entropy
Lyapunov functional — into which all three regimes naturally embed, while
identifying the irreducibly different *kinematic geometries* (vector flow
in phase space vs. scalar aggregate vs. discrete simplex) that prevent a
fully uniform representation.

Notation: $\partial_t$ for time derivative, $\nabla_r$ for spatial
gradient, $\nabla_p$ or $\nabla_k$ for momentum/crystal-momentum gradient,
$\delta / \delta x$ for functional derivative, $\langle \cdot \rangle$ for
ensemble average, $\beta = 1/(k_B T)$.

---

## 1. Transport regime

The transport regime asks: *how do conserved quantities — charge,
particle number, momentum, energy — flow through the crystal when
intensive variables (electrochemical potential, temperature, stress)
vary in space?* The mathematical core is a kinetic equation for a
phase-space distribution function, plus linear-response transport
coefficients that contract that distribution into macroscopic currents.

### 1.1 Mathematical formalism — Boltzmann transport equation and the Kubo response

The canonical object is the **semiclassical distribution function**
$f_n(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{k}, t)$ for each carrier band $n$, defined on
the six-dimensional phase space (position $\mathbf{r}$, crystal momentum
$\mathbf{k}$). Its evolution is the **Boltzmann transport equation**:

$$
\partial_t f_n + \mathbf{v}_n(\mathbf{k}) \cdot \nabla_r f_n
+ \tfrac{1}{\hbar} \mathbf{F} \cdot \nabla_k f_n
= \mathcal{C}[f]_n,
$$

where $\mathbf{v}_n(\mathbf{k}) = \hbar^{-1}\nabla_k \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k})$
is the band group velocity, $\mathbf{F} = -e(\mathbf{E} + \mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B})$
is the external (Lorentz) force, and $\mathcal{C}[f]$ is the collision
integral encoding scattering. In standard Fermi-golden-rule form:

$$
\mathcal{C}[f]_n(\mathbf{k}) = \sum_{n', \mathbf{k}'} \left[
W_{n'\mathbf{k}' \to n \mathbf{k}} f_{n'}(\mathbf{k}')(1 - f_n(\mathbf{k}))
- W_{n\mathbf{k} \to n'\mathbf{k}'} f_n(\mathbf{k})(1 - f_{n'}(\mathbf{k}'))
\right],
$$

with detailed balance ensuring the equilibrium $f_n^{0}(\mathbf{k}) = \{ \exp[\beta(\varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k}) - \mu)] + 1\}^{-1}$
(Fermi–Dirac) is a stationary point. For ionic/lattice transport one
replaces Fermi–Dirac with Maxwell–Boltzmann for classical ions or
Bose–Einstein for phonons.

The most widely used simplification — and what `BoltzTraP`-style
codes actually solve — is the **relaxation-time approximation** (RTA):

$$
\mathcal{C}[f]_n \;\approx\; -\frac{f_n(\mathbf{k}) - f_n^{0}(\mathbf{k})}{\tau_n(\mathbf{k})},
$$

where $\tau_n(\mathbf{k})$ is a state-resolved transport lifetime.

Equivalently, **the Kubo formula** gives the same transport coefficients
in linear response without postulating a kinetic equation. Specializing
the general Kubo bilinear to the current operator $\hat{j}_\alpha$ gives
the conductivity

$$
\sigma_{\alpha\beta}(\omega) = \frac{1}{V} \int_0^\infty dt\, e^{i\omega t}\,\langle \hat{j}_\alpha(t) \hat{j}_\beta(0) \rangle_{\rm eq},
$$

(Kubo–Greenwood) and the heat-current correlation gives the thermal
conductivity (Green–Kubo)

$$
\kappa_{\alpha\beta} = \frac{1}{V k_B T^2} \int_0^\infty dt\, \langle \hat{J}^Q_\alpha(t) \hat{J}^Q_\beta(0) \rangle_{\rm eq}.
$$

The equivalence of the linearized BTE-RTA and Kubo–Greenwood for
non-interacting carriers is a textbook result, derived rigorously from
quantum many-body theory via the Wigner function / Dyson-equation
gradient expansion.

For ionic transport in semiconductor / battery contexts, the
**drift-diffusion (Poisson–Nernst–Planck) system** is the fluid-limit
reduction of the BTE:

$$
\partial_t n_i + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{j}_i = G_i - R_i, \quad
\mathbf{j}_i = \mu_i n_i \mathbf{E} - D_i \nabla n_i, \quad
\nabla \cdot (\varepsilon \nabla \phi) = -\rho,
$$

with the **Einstein relation** $D_i = \mu_i k_B T / q_i$ enforcing
consistency.

### 1.2 State at instant $t$

The irreducible state is the family of distribution functions:

- $\{ f_n(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{k}, t) \}_n$, real-valued on
  $\mathbf{r} \in \Omega$ and $\mathbf{k} \in \text{BZ}$. Dimensionless
  occupation in $[0,1]$ (fermions) or $[0,\infty)$ (bosons).
- For phonon transport: $\{ n_\lambda(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{q}, t) \}_\lambda$
  with Bose–Einstein equilibrium $n^0_\lambda = [\exp(\beta\hbar\omega_\lambda(\mathbf{q})) - 1]^{-1}$.
- For ionic transport (drift-diffusion): scalar densities $\{n_i(\mathbf{r}, t)\}_i$
  per species + electrostatic potential $\phi(\mathbf{r}, t)$ (constrained
  by Poisson, not independent).

**Derived (not state)**: number densities, current densities, energy
density, stress, etc. All are *contractions* of $f$ against an operator
and the band-structure tabulation.

### 1.3 Equations of motion — discretized BTE

Discretized on a tetrahedron / uniform $\mathbf{k}$-mesh and a real-space
grid:

$$
\partial_t f_{n, \mathbf{r}_a, \mathbf{k}_b}
+ (\mathbf{v}_n(\mathbf{k}_b) \cdot \nabla_r f)_{a,b}
+ \tfrac{1}{\hbar}(\mathbf{F} \cdot \nabla_k f)_{a,b}
= \mathcal{C}[\{f_{n', a, b'}\}]_{n, a, b}.
$$

The collision integral is local in $\mathbf{r}$ but nonlocal in
$\mathbf{k}$. Time stepping: explicit (BGK / RTA) or implicit iterative
(full collision integral). In steady state, the BTE becomes a linear
matrix problem for linear-response coefficients.

### 1.4 Conservation laws and constraints

- **Particle / charge number**: $\partial_t n + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{j} = 0$.
  Exact consequence of $\int \mathcal{C}[f]\,d^3k = 0$.
- **Momentum / energy**: in clean systems, collision operators must satisfy
  $\int \hbar\mathbf{k}\,\mathcal{C}[f]\,d^3k = 0$ and
  $\int \varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k})\,\mathcal{C}[f]\,d^3k = 0$.
- **Onsager reciprocity**: $L_{\alpha\beta}(\mathbf{B}) = L_{\beta\alpha}(-\mathbf{B})$,
  from time-reversal symmetry of microscopic dynamics.
- **Positivity / H-theorem**: entropy $S[f] = -k_B \sum_n \int \{f_n \ln f_n + (1-f_n)\ln(1-f_n)\}\,d^3r\,d^3k$
  satisfies $dS/dt \geq 0$ under the collision operator. Equality only at
  local equilibrium.
- **Positivity of transport coefficients**: $\sigma, \kappa, \boldsymbol\mu$
  as matrices are PSD, enforced by the bilinear-form structure of Kubo.
- **Bound on $f$**: $0 \leq f_n \leq 1$ (fermions), $n_\lambda \geq 0$ (bosons).
- **Detailed balance**: $W_{i\to j}\,f^0_i(1 - f^0_j) = W_{j\to i}\,f^0_j(1 - f^0_i)$
  at equilibrium.

### 1.5 Standard observables

| Observable | Reads from | Operation |
|---|---|---|
| Carrier density $n(\mathbf{r}, t)$ | $f$ | $\int f \, d^3k/(2\pi)^3$ |
| Current density $\mathbf{j}(\mathbf{r}, t)$ | $f$, $\mathbf{v}_n$ | $-e\sum_n \int \mathbf{v}_n f\,d^3k/(2\pi)^3$ |
| Conductivity $\sigma_{\alpha\beta}$ | $f^0$ (linear response) | $e^2 \sum_n \int \tau_n v_{n\alpha} v_{n\beta}\,(-\partial f^0/\partial\varepsilon)\,d^3k/(2\pi)^3$ |
| Mobility $\mu_{\alpha\beta}$ | $\sigma$ | $\sigma_{\alpha\beta}/(en)$ |
| Seebeck $S_{\alpha\beta}$ | $f^0$, $\varepsilon - \mu$ | weighted $\sigma$-like integral |
| Thermal conductivity $\kappa_{\alpha\beta}$ | $f^0$, $\varepsilon - \mu$, $\tau$ | second-moment Kubo integral |
| Ionic diffusivity $D_i$ | $\mu_i$, $T$ | $\mu_i k_B T / q_i$ (Einstein) |
| Migration barrier $E_a^i$ | NEB (structural / chemical) | from chemical regime |

### 1.6 Couplings to other regimes

- **Electronic**: $\mathbf{v}_n(\mathbf{k})$, $\varepsilon_n(\mathbf{k})$
  from band structure; $f^0$ depends on $\mu$, $T$; DOS near $\mu$ sets $\sigma$.
- **Structural / mechanical**: scattering rates depend on defect density,
  strain (deformation-potential scattering), ionic positions (e-ph matrix elements).
- **Thermal**: $T$ enters $f^0$; phonon distributions $n_\lambda$ are
  transport state; e-ph coupling is bidirectional energy exchange (Joule
  heating, Peltier).
- **Chemical/surface**: ionic migration barriers from the chemical regime
  set $D_i$; surface boundary conditions on $f$.
- **Magnetic**: $\mathbf{B}$ in Lorentz force generates Hall components
  $\sigma_{\alpha\beta}(\mathbf{B})$; switches Onsager symmetry to
  $L(\mathbf{B}) = L^T(-\mathbf{B})$.

### 1.7 Numerical methods

`BoltzTraP` / `BoltzTraP2` (Fourier interpolation of band energies +
linear-response RTA); `EPW` (Wannier interpolation of e-ph coupling);
`AMSET` (mode-resolved scattering); NEMD for thermal transport;
Green–Kubo on equilibrium MD trajectories. Sparsity: collision matrix
local in $\mathbf{r}$ but dense in $\mathbf{k}$; sparsity hints come
from concentration near the Fermi surface for transport.

## 2. Thermodynamic regime

The thermodynamic regime asks: *given a structural / electronic
configuration at temperature $T$, what is its free energy, and which
phases are stable?* The mathematical core is a partition function
$Z(T, V, N)$ producing thermodynamic potentials, plus a convex-analysis
construction (the convex hull of formation energies vs. composition)
that decides stability.

### 2.1 Mathematical formalism — partition function, free energy, convex hull

For a system with Hamiltonian $\hat{H}$ at temperature $T$, the
**canonical partition function** is

$$
Z(T, V, N) = \mathrm{Tr}\,e^{-\beta \hat{H}} = \sum_i e^{-\beta E_i}.
$$

The **Helmholtz free energy** is

$$
F(T, V, N) = -k_B T \ln Z(T, V, N),
$$

with differentials $dF = -S\,dT - P\,dV + \mu\,dN$ giving

$$
S = -(\partial F/\partial T)_{V,N}, \quad P = -(\partial F/\partial V)_{T,N}, \quad \mu = (\partial F/\partial N)_{T,V}.
$$

The **Gibbs free energy** $G(T, P, N) = F + PV$ and **grand potential**
$\Omega(T, V, \mu) = F - \mu N$ are Legendre transforms.

For a crystal at moderate temperature the dominant contribution beyond
DFT total energy is **harmonic phonon vibrational free energy**:

$$
F_{\rm vib}(T) = \tfrac{1}{2} \sum_{\mathbf{q}, \lambda} \hbar \omega_\lambda(\mathbf{q})
+ k_B T \sum_{\mathbf{q}, \lambda} \ln\!\big(1 - e^{-\beta \hbar \omega_\lambda(\mathbf{q})}\big),
$$

so $F(T, V) \approx E_0(V) + F_{\rm vib}(T, V) + F_{\rm el}(T, V)$.

**Phase stability via the convex hull**: for composition
$\mathbf{x} = (x_1, \ldots, x_K)$ on the $(K-1)$-simplex, gather formation
Gibbs energies $g_\phi(\mathbf{x}, T, P)$ of all candidate phases.
Equilibrium free-energy surface = **lower convex envelope**:

$$
g^*(\mathbf{x}, T, P) = \min_{\{c_\phi \geq 0,\,\sum c_\phi \mathbf{x}_\phi = \mathbf{x},\,\sum c_\phi = 1\}}\sum_\phi c_\phi\,g_\phi(\mathbf{x}_\phi, T, P).
$$

Phase $\phi$ is *stable* iff $(\mathbf{x}_\phi, g_\phi)$ lies *on* the hull;
$E_{\rm hull}(\phi) = g_\phi - g^*(\mathbf{x}_\phi) \geq 0$ is the "energy
above hull". **Common-tangent construction** identifies two-phase coexistence.
This is the CALPHAD method's mathematical core.

### 2.2 State at instant $t$

The thermodynamic regime, unlike the others, is *aggregate / zero-dimensional*
in its native form. State at instant $t$:

- **Intensive scalars**: $T(t)$, $P(t)$ (or $V(t)$), $\{\mu_i(t)\}$.
- **Composition**: mole fractions $\{x_i(t)\}$ on the simplex.
- **Phase amounts**: $\{c_\phi(t)\}$ with $\sum c_\phi = 1$, $c_\phi \geq 0$.

In the spatially-extended (phase-field) generalization, this becomes a
field $c_\phi(\mathbf{r}, t)$ or order parameter $\eta_\phi(\mathbf{r}, t)$.

Thermodynamic potentials ($F$, $G$, $S$, etc.) are **functions** $F(T, V, \mathbf{x})$
tabulated or fitted (CALPHAD) per phase — part of the *model*, not the state.

### 2.3 Equations of motion / dynamics

At equilibrium, thermodynamic state is *static* — dynamics is approach to
equilibrium. Two canonical dynamic laws:

**Cahn–Hilliard** (conserved order parameter):

$$
\partial_t c(\mathbf{r}, t) = \nabla \cdot \big( M(c)\,\nabla \frac{\delta \mathcal{F}[c]}{\delta c} \big),\quad \mathcal{F}[c] = \int_\Omega [f(c) + \tfrac{\kappa}{2}|\nabla c|^2]\,d^3r.
$$

**Allen–Cahn** (non-conserved order parameter):

$$
\partial_t \eta(\mathbf{r}, t) = -L\,\frac{\delta \mathcal{F}[\eta]}{\delta \eta}.
$$

Both are **gradient flows** of a Ginzburg–Landau-type free-energy functional
with monotone decay $d\mathcal{F}/dt \leq 0$ — $\mathcal{F}$ is a Lyapunov
functional.

More generally, the **Onsager / Mori–Zwanzig** form near equilibrium is

$$
\partial_t x_i = -\sum_j L_{ij}\,\frac{\delta \mathcal{F}}{\delta x_j} + \xi_i(t),
$$

with $L_{ij}$ symmetric PSD (Onsager) and $\xi$ a fluctuating force
satisfying $\langle \xi_i(t) \xi_j(t') \rangle = 2 k_B T L_{ij}\,\delta(t - t')$.

### 2.4 Conservation laws and constraints

- **Convexity** of free energy (or its lower convex envelope) — global
  thermodynamic-consistency constraint.
- **Maxwell relations** from $d^2 F = 0$:
  $(\partial S/\partial V)_T = (\partial P/\partial T)_V$ and cyclic analogues.
- **Gibbs–Duhem**: $\sum_i N_i\,d\mu_i = -S\,dT + V\,dP$.
- **Third law**: $S \to 0$ as $T \to 0$ (with ground-state degeneracy).
- **Second law / H-theorem**: total entropy non-decreasing; for closed
  systems with driving, free energy monotonically non-increasing toward
  minimum at fixed $(T, V, N)$ — Lyapunov.
- **Stoichiometric constraints**: mole fractions sum to 1; species mass
  / atomic-number balances under reaction.
- **Composition simplex**: $x_i \in [0, 1]$, $\sum_i x_i = 1$.
- **Positivity of phase amounts**: $c_\phi \geq 0$.

### 2.5 Standard observables

| Observable | Reads from | Operation |
|---|---|---|
| Total energy $U$ | $\hat{H}$ via DFT | electronic structure (other regime) |
| Formation energy $\Delta H_f$ | $E_0$ of compound + references | $E_0^{\rm cmpd} - \sum_i n_i E_0^{\rm elem}$ |
| Free energy $F(T, V, N)$ | partition function | $-k_B T \ln Z$ |
| Entropy $S$ | $F$ | $-\partial F/\partial T$ |
| Pressure $P$ | $F$ | $-\partial F/\partial V$ |
| Chemical potential $\mu_i$ | $F$ | $\partial F/\partial N_i$ |
| Heat capacity $C_V$ | $F$ | $-T\,\partial^2 F/\partial T^2$ |
| Phase stability $E_{\rm hull}$ | $\{g_\phi\}$ | distance above convex hull |
| Phase boundaries | $g^*$ | common-tangent construction |

### 2.6 Couplings to other regimes

- **Structural**: $E_0$ and harmonic Hessian (→ $\omega_\lambda(\mathbf{q})$)
  from structure. Phase transformations *change* structure.
- **Thermal (phonon)**: $F_{\rm vib}$ dominant finite-temperature contribution.
- **Electronic**: $F_{\rm el}(T)$ from Fermi–Dirac filling of band structure;
  magnetic ordering contributes $F_{\rm mag}$.
- **Chemical/surface**: surface free energy $\gamma$ is the thermodynamic
  regime's restriction to a 2D manifold; adsorption changes grand potential.
- **Transport**: Onsager $L_{ij}$ computed using *equilibrium* fluctuations
  (Green–Kubo / fluctuation–dissipation).

### 2.7 Numerical methods

CALPHAD; finite-temperature DFT; QHA; self-consistent phonons (SCP);
thermodynamic integration; Wang–Landau sampling; lattice Monte Carlo /
cluster expansion for configurational entropy; free-energy perturbation /
umbrella sampling for reaction-coordinate free energies; convex-hull
algorithms (Quickhull) for phase-stability post-processing.

## 3. Chemical / surface regime

The chemical/surface regime asks: *how do atoms or molecules attach to a
surface, rearrange along reaction coordinates, and convert reactants to
products?* The mathematical core is (i) a reaction coordinate $\xi$ along
a minimum-energy path, (ii) a transition-state-theory rate constant $k(T)$
for crossing the barrier, and (iii) coupled rate equations or a master
equation over surface microstates.

### 3.1 Mathematical formalism — NEB, TST, rate equations, BEP

The **potential-energy surface (PES)** $V(\{\mathbf{R}_I\})$ over atomic
positions has minima (reactants, products) connected by a **minimum
energy path (MEP)** — a curve $\gamma(s)$ in configuration space along
which $\nabla V$ is parallel to the path tangent except at the saddle.

**Nudged elastic band (NEB)** discretizes the path into a band of $N$
images $\{\mathbf{R}_i\}_{i=0}^{N+1}$ with springs and projected forces:

$$
\mathbf{F}_i^{\rm NEB} = -\nabla V(\mathbf{R}_i)\big|_\perp + \mathbf{F}_i^{\rm spring}\big|_\parallel.
$$

The **climbing-image** variant for the highest-energy image:

$$
\mathbf{F}_{i^*}^{\rm CI} = -\nabla V(\mathbf{R}_{i^*}) + 2(\nabla V(\mathbf{R}_{i^*})\cdot \hat{\boldsymbol\tau}_{i^*})\,\hat{\boldsymbol\tau}_{i^*},
$$

driving it uphill along $\hat{\boldsymbol\tau}$ — converges to the exact
saddle point (one negative Hessian eigenvalue along $\hat{\boldsymbol\tau}$).

**Transition state theory (TST)** gives the forward rate:

$$
k_{\rm TST}(T) = \frac{k_B T}{h}\,\frac{Q^{\ddagger}}{Q^{\rm R}}\,\exp\!\big(-E_a / k_B T\big).
$$

In **harmonic TST (hTST) / Vineyard** form:

$$
k_{\rm hTST}(T) = \frac{\prod_{i=1}^{3N} \nu_i^{\rm min}}{\prod_{j=1}^{3N-1} \nu_j^{\ddagger}}\,\exp\!\big(-E_a / k_B T\big).
$$

For **electron-transfer reactions**, **Marcus theory**:

$$
k_{\rm ET}(T) = A \exp\!\big[-(\Delta G^\circ + \lambda)^2 / (4\lambda k_B T)\big],
$$

with $\lambda$ the reorganization energy and $\Delta G^\circ$ the driving
force — gives the normal and inverted regions of electron-transfer kinetics.

**Brønsted–Evans–Polanyi (BEP)** relations:

$$
E_a = \alpha\,\Delta E_{\rm rxn} + \beta, \qquad 0 < \alpha < 1.
$$

Combined with **Sabatier's principle** (optimal activity at moderate
binding) gives the volcano-plot prediction of catalytic activity vs.
adsorption-energy descriptor.

**Microkinetic (rate-equation) model**:

$$
\frac{d\theta_i}{dt} = \sum_{r=1}^{R} \nu_{ir}\big( k_r^{+} \prod_j \theta_j^{\nu^-_{jr}} - k_r^{-} \prod_j \theta_j^{\nu^+_{jr}}\big),
$$

with $\nu_{ir}$ stoichiometric coefficients and rate constants $k_r^{\pm}$
satisfying detailed balance $k_r^+/k_r^- = \exp(-\Delta G_r / k_B T)$.

For coverage-resolved kinetics, the **lattice-gas master equation**:

$$
\frac{dP(C, t)}{dt} = \sum_{C' \neq C}[W(C' \to C)P(C', t) - W(C \to C')P(C, t)],
$$

with detailed balance $W(C \to C')\,P_{\rm eq}(C) = W(C' \to C)\,P_{\rm eq}(C')$.

### 3.2 State at instant $t$

Three natural representations:

- **Mean-field / microkinetic**: surface coverages
  $\boldsymbol\theta(t) = (\theta_1(t), \ldots, \theta_S(t))$ with site-balance
  constraint $\sum_i \theta_i + \theta_{\rm free} = 1$. Optionally augmented
  by reaction extents $\xi_r(t)$.
- **Lattice-gas / kMC**: occupation variables $\sigma_a \in \{0, 1, \ldots, S\}$
  on each lattice site $a$; full configuration $\{\sigma_a\}_{a=1}^{N_{\rm sites}}$
  evolving stochastically.
- **Defect concentrations**: $\{c_d(\mathbf{r}, t)\}_d$ for each defect type.

**Derived (not state)**: reaction rates, turnover frequency, adsorption
energies, transition state energies, surface energies — all computed from
PES (structural) + partition functions (thermodynamic).

### 3.3 Equations of motion

Rate equations $d\boldsymbol\theta/dt = \mathbf{S}(\boldsymbol\theta; \mathbf{k})$
in the mean-field/continuum limit; master equation $dP/dt = \mathbf{W}P$ in
the discrete-state limit; kMC realizations sample master-equation trajectories.

For reaction-coordinate sampling, the **Langevin equation** along $\xi$:

$$
m_\xi \ddot\xi = -\frac{\partial G(\xi)}{\partial \xi} - \gamma_\xi \dot\xi + \zeta(t),\quad \langle\zeta(t)\zeta(t')\rangle = 2\gamma_\xi k_B T\,\delta(t-t').
$$

### 3.4 Conservation laws and constraints

- **Atomic mass / element balance**: each step conserves every element.
  The stoichiometry matrix $\boldsymbol\nu$ has a left null space corresponding
  to conserved atomic moieties.
- **Site balance**: $\sum_i \theta_i + \theta_{\rm free} = 1$ — hard simplex.
- **Detailed balance**: $k_r^+/k_r^- = \exp(-\Delta G_r / k_B T)$.
- **Wegscheider conditions**: in cyclic networks, products of equilibrium
  constants around any cycle must equal 1 (thermodynamic consistency of
  independent rate constants).
- **Non-negativity**: $\theta_i \geq 0$, $k_r^\pm \geq 0$, $P(C) \geq 0$.
- **BEP linear-scaling consistency** (when imposed): $E_a$ vs.
  $\Delta E_{\rm rxn}$ lie on the posited line — soft constraint useful
  for screening.

### 3.5 Standard observables

| Observable | Reads from | Operation |
|---|---|---|
| Adsorption energy $E_{\rm ads}$ | structure + electronic | $E_{\rm surf+ads} - E_{\rm surf} - E_{\rm ads,gas}$ |
| Surface energy $\gamma$ | electronic + structural | $(E_{\rm slab} - n E_{\rm bulk})/(2A)$ |
| Activation barrier $E_a$ | NEB on PES | $V^{\ddagger} - V^{\rm min}$ |
| Rate constant $k(T)$ | $E_a$, vibrational spectrum | hTST formula |
| Reaction profile $V(\xi)$ | NEB | path energy |
| Turnover frequency (TOF) | rate equations | steady-state solution |
| Catalytic activity descriptor | $\theta$, $E_{\rm ads}$ | volcano-plot scalar |
| Degree of rate control $X_{RC,i}$ | microkinetic | $(k_i/r)(\partial r/\partial k_i)$ |
| Selectivity | branching ratios | $r_A/(r_A + r_B)$ |

### 3.6 Couplings to other regimes

- **Structural**: PES, NEB paths, surface terminations, defect geometries
  all live in structural regime.
- **Electronic**: adsorption energies, surface dipoles, work function,
  charge transfer; Marcus theory directly couples ET kinetics to electronic
  structure.
- **Thermal**: $T$ in Arrhenius / hTST; vibrational entropy of adsorbates.
- **Transport**: surface diffusion barriers feed ionic diffusivity $D_i$.
- **Thermodynamic**: surface free energy $\gamma$ is the surface
  restriction of the thermodynamic potential; Langmuir adsorption isotherms
  come from grand-canonical formulation.

### 3.7 Numerical methods

NEB / climbing-image NEB / dimer / string method for saddles; hTST /
variational TST / ring-polymer instanton for rates; kMC for stochastic
surface evolution; mean-field microkinetic ODE integrators; Campbell
degree-of-rate-control; grand-canonical MC for adsorption isotherms;
Wang–Landau for adsorption phase diagrams; scaling-relation surrogate
models for catalyst screening.

## 4. Cross-regime synthesis

### 4.1 Shared structure: distributions over a state space with a Lyapunov functional

All three regimes are **statistical**. Each carries a distribution over a
microstate manifold:

- **Transport**: $f_n(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{k}, t)$ over single-particle
  phase space.
- **Thermodynamic**: Boltzmann measure $e^{-\beta E_i}/Z$ over configurations
  (and in phase-field, a coarse-grained order-parameter field).
- **Chemical/surface**: master-equation measure $P(C, t)$ over microstates
  (and in mean-field, the coverage vector $\boldsymbol\theta(t)$).

Each regime has a **scalar Lyapunov functional**:

| Regime | Lyapunov | Direction |
|---|---|---|
| Transport | $-S[f] = k_B\int(f\ln f + (1-f)\ln(1-f))$ | non-increasing (H-theorem: $dS/dt \ge 0$) |
| Thermodynamic | $\mathcal{F}[c]$ (Helmholtz / Ginzburg–Landau) | non-increasing |
| Chemical/surface | $\mathcal{F}_{\rm micro}[P] = \sum_C P(C)\ln(P(C)/P_{\rm eq}(C))$ | non-increasing |

Equilibrium = unique minimizer of the Lyapunov functional under conserved
quantities; dynamics is dissipative *toward* that minimizer.

### 4.2 The deepest common canonical: GENERIC two-bracket structure

The General Equation for Non-Equilibrium Reversible-Irreversible Coupling
(GENERIC) of Grmela and Öttinger provides a single canonical form:

$$
\frac{dx}{dt} = L(x)\,\frac{\delta E}{\delta x} + M(x)\,\frac{\delta S}{\delta x},
$$

with **degeneracy conditions**

$$
L(x)\,\frac{\delta S}{\delta x} = 0,\qquad M(x)\,\frac{\delta E}{\delta x} = 0,
$$

$L$ antisymmetric (Poisson bracket, Jacobi), $M$ symmetric PSD (dissipative
bracket). Equivalently for observables:

$$
\frac{dA}{dt} = \{A, E\} + [A, S].
$$

**How each regime sits in GENERIC**:

- **Transport (BTE)**: $x = f$, $E = \int \varepsilon\,f\,d^3k\,d^3r$,
  $S = -k_B\int[f\ln f + (1-f)\ln(1-f)]$; $L$ is the Poisson structure
  (streaming + force); $M$ is the collision operator (relaxation).
  Onsager $L_{\alpha\beta} = L_{\beta\alpha}$ is a direct consequence of $M = M^T$.
- **Thermodynamic / phase-field**: $x = c(\mathbf{r})$; $E$ constant at
  this coarse-grained level; dynamics is pure dissipative:
  $\partial_t c = \nabla \cdot M\nabla(\delta \mathcal{F}/\delta c)$ —
  exactly $M\,\delta S/\delta x$ with $S = -\mathcal{F}/T$ at fixed $T$.
- **Chemical/surface**: $x = P(C)$ or $\boldsymbol\theta$; $E$ encoded in
  state energies; master equation $dP/dt = WP$ with detailed balance is a
  dissipative gradient flow of relative entropy $D(P \| P_{\rm eq})$ —
  canonical example of $M\,\delta S/\delta x$ on a discrete state space.

The **degeneracy** $M\,\delta E/\delta x = 0$ ensures energy conservation
by dissipation — heat/mass/charge moves but total energy stays constant —
exactly the conservation law enforced by BTE collisions, common-tangent
(no free-energy decrease at coexistence), and rate-equation stoichiometry.

**Positivity of $L$/$M$** is the single most important shared constraint:
$\sigma$, $\kappa$, $D$, $\mathbf{W}^{\rm sym}$, Onsager $L_{ij}$, and the
dissipative bracket $M$ are all PSD as a direct consequence of the second
law. A PINO loss term enforcing $L \succeq 0$ enforces all three regimes'
positivity requirements at once.

### 4.3 What's irreducibly different — kinematic geometry

GENERIC unifies the *form* of the dynamics, but the three regimes differ
irreducibly in the **kinematic geometry** of their state spaces:

- **Transport** state lives on 6D phase space $\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{k}$
  per band. Dynamics is *vector flow* (streaming + force + collision).
- **Thermodynamic** state, in native zero-dimensional form, is *scalar
  aggregates* $(T, V, \mathbf{x}, \{c_\phi\})$. Spatialized → scalar fields
  on real space. Dynamics is *gradient flow* of a scalar functional.
- **Chemical/surface** state is *discrete* on the simplex
  $\boldsymbol\theta \in \Delta^S$ (or discrete configuration $\{\sigma_a\}$
  on a lattice). Dynamics is *combinatorial*. Detailed balance is a
  graph-theoretic condition (Wegscheider cycles).

Summary:

| Regime | State type | Index | Dynamics |
|---|---|---|---|
| Transport | distribution $f$ | continuous BZ × space | hyperbolic + dissipative PDE |
| Thermodynamic | scalar fields / aggregates | space (or 0D) | parabolic gradient-flow PDE |
| Chemical | coverages / configurations | discrete species × sites | ODE / Markov / SDE |

These are *not* unifiable into a single tensor representation without loss.
A library that treats all three should expect three distinct state schemas,
three distinct discretizations, and a *common* residual contract on top:
each regime contributes (i) state-vector portion, (ii) dynamics residuals
$\mathcal{R}(x, \dot x; T, \ldots) = 0$, (iii) sign-constrained conservation /
positivity residuals, (iv) algebraic identities (Maxwell, Einstein, detailed
balance, BEP).

### 4.4 Cross-couplings within and beyond group C

**Within group:**

- **Transport ↔ Thermodynamic**: Green–Kubo derives transport coefficients
  from equilibrium fluctuations; local Fermi–Dirac with $(T(\mathbf{r}), \mu(\mathbf{r}))$
  is BTE's local-equilibrium target.
- **Thermodynamic ↔ Chemical**: rate constants $k_r^\pm$ constrained by
  $\Delta G_r$; adsorption isotherms from grand-canonical; phase stability
  constrains physical surface terminations.
- **Transport ↔ Chemical**: ionic diffusivity $D_i = \mu_i k_B T / q$ set
  by hTST barrier crossings.

**Out of group:**

- **To electronic**: every regime depends on it. Transport reads
  band structure and velocities. Thermodynamic reads $E_0$ and electronic
  free energy. Chemical reads bonding energies, work functions, Marcus
  reorganization energies.
- **To structural**: thermodynamic phase stability defined
  relative to candidate crystals; chemical reactions are atom permutations;
  PES on which NEB runs is a structural-coordinate manifold.
- **To thermal**: $T$ is the basic intensive parameter; phonon
  free energy $F_{\rm vib}$ largest finite-T correction; e-ph scattering
  dominant resistive mechanism.
- **To mechanical**: stress $\sigma_{\alpha\beta}$ as derivative
  of $F$ couples thermodynamic to mechanical; strain modifies PES; deformation-
  potential scattering for transport.
- **To magnetic**: $\mathbf{B}$ in Lorentz force; magnetic order
  $F_{\rm mag}$; spin-state-resolved reaction barriers.
- **To optical**: $\sigma_{\alpha\beta}(\omega)$ same Kubo–Greenwood
  object — transport IS the optical regime in dynamical-response form.

### 4.5 Summary: shape of a unified residual contract

If `/physics` provides PINO-style residuals across all three regimes through
a single contract, the residuals cluster into:

1. **EOM residuals**: discretized BTE, gradient flow, rate / master equation.
2. **Conservation residuals**: $\partial_t n + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{j} = 0$;
   $\sum_\phi c_\phi = 1$; $\sum_i \theta_i = 1$; element balance from
   stoichiometry; Maxwell-relation closure.
3. **Positivity / inequality residuals**: $f \in [0, 1]$; $\theta_i \in [0,1]$;
   $L \succeq 0$; $\sigma \succeq 0$; $\kappa \succeq 0$; $dS/dt \geq 0$.
4. **Algebraic identities**: $D = \mu k_B T / q$ (Einstein); $k_+/k_- = \exp(-\Delta G/k_B T)$
   (detailed balance); $L_{ij}(\mathbf{B}) = L_{ji}(-\mathbf{B})$ (Onsager);
   common-tangent for coexistence; BEP linear-scaling consistency.

The GENERIC two-bracket form gives all four cleanly: EOM is
$\dot x = L\,\delta E/\delta x + M\,\delta S/\delta x$; conservation is
antisymmetry of $L$ plus $M\,\delta E/\delta x = 0$; positivity is $M \succeq 0$
plus the Jacobi identity for $L$; identities follow from explicit forms of
$E$, $S$, $L$, $M$.
