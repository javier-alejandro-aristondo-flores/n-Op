# Group A — Ion Dynamics: Structural, Mechanical, Thermal

> **Notation.** Phonon branch index: `s` in this file ≡ `ν` (e-ph passages here and in group-B) ≡ `λ` (group-C) — one object, three historical symbols. κ here is the **phonon (lattice)** part only; the observable-level total is `κ_total = κ_el + κ_ph` (arch-10 `combineTol` example).

Deep mathematical research for the n-Op `/physics` library. Scope: the three
regimes whose primary degrees of freedom are the **ions** (nuclei) and the
**lattice cell** that contains them. All three share a common substrate: the
crystal as a periodic point set indexed over a Bravais lattice.

The three regimes are not three different state spaces — they are three
different *Hamiltonians* (or *functionals*) defined over what is essentially
the same configuration manifold. This is the central observation that
motivates the synthesis at the end.

Notation conventions:

- $\mathbf R_I$, $I = 1,\dots,N$: position of atom $I$ in Cartesian space.
- $\mathbf h$: the $3\times 3$ matrix of cell vectors, columns $\mathbf a_1,
  \mathbf a_2, \mathbf a_3$. The cell volume is $\Omega = \det \mathbf h$.
- Reduced (crystal) coordinates $\mathbf s_I \in [0,1)^3$ satisfy
  $\mathbf R_I = \mathbf h \, \mathbf s_I$.
- $\boldsymbol\epsilon$: small strain tensor; $\boldsymbol\eta$: finite
  (Green-Lagrange) strain.
- $\mathbf q$, branch index $s$: phonon wavevector and branch.
- $\hbar, k_B$: Planck and Boltzmann constants.

## Regime 1 — Structural

The regime that fixes "where atoms are" — the geometric ground truth on which
every other regime is layered.

### 1.1 Mathematical formalism

The canonical object is the **Born-Oppenheimer (BO) total-energy functional**

$$
E_{\rm BO}(\{\mathbf R_I\}, \mathbf h) \;=\; \min_{\Psi_e} \Big\langle \Psi_e
\Big|\, \hat T_e + \hat V_{ee} + \hat V_{eI}(\{\mathbf R_I\}, \mathbf h)
\Big| \Psi_e \Big\rangle \;+\; V_{II}(\{\mathbf R_I\}, \mathbf h)
$$

where the minimisation is over electronic many-body states $\Psi_e$ at fixed
nuclear configuration. In Kohn-Sham DFT this reduces to

$$
E_{\rm KS}[n;\,\mathbf R,\mathbf h] = T_s[n] + \int v_{\rm ext}(\mathbf r;
\mathbf R)\,n(\mathbf r)\,d\mathbf r + E_H[n] + E_{xc}[n] + V_{II},
$$

with $n(\mathbf r)$ self-consistent at each $(\mathbf R, \mathbf h)$. The
function $E_{\rm BO} : (\mathbb R^{3N} \times \mathrm{GL}^+(3)) \to \mathbb
R$ is, by construction, smooth almost everywhere except at conical
intersections of electronic surfaces, which the regime excludes.

This functional is the **structural regime's canonical object**: every
structural observable is either a stationary point of $E_{\rm BO}$ or a
derivative of it.

### 1.2 State representation at instant $t$

The independent degrees of freedom are:

- $\{\mathbf R_I(t)\}_{I=1}^{N} \in \mathbb R^{3N}$ — atom positions.
- $\mathbf h(t) \in \mathrm{GL}^+(3) \subset \mathbb R^{3\times 3}$ — cell
  matrix (9 reals; with proper-orientation constraint $\det \mathbf h > 0$).
- $\{Z_I\}_{I=1}^N$ — atomic numbers (discrete, time-independent labels;
  technically state, but immutable under the dynamics considered here).

Total real-valued DOFs at instant $t$: $3N + 9$. With the symmetry that the
total energy is invariant under global rotation of $(\mathbf R, \mathbf h)$
and under choice of Bravais lattice basis (basis transformations
$\mathbf h \to \mathbf h \mathbf M$, $\mathbf M \in \mathrm{GL}(3,\mathbb
Z)$ with $|\det \mathbf M|=1$), the **physical** DOF count is $3N + 6$:
either six cell parameters $(a,b,c,\alpha,\beta,\gamma)$ or six independent
components of the symmetric metric $\mathbf g = \mathbf h^\top \mathbf h$.

If we promote the configuration to a phase space for time evolution we add
conjugate momenta $\mathbf P_I$ and a cell momentum $\boldsymbol\Pi_{\mathbf
h}$ (Parrinello-Rahman); the doubled phase space has dimension $2(3N+6)$.

What is **state**: $(\mathbf R_I, \mathbf h)$ and (for dynamics) their
conjugate momenta plus the species labels $Z_I$.

What is **derived**: the electronic density $n(\mathbf r)$, the BO energy
$E_{\rm BO}$, forces, stresses, and every aggregate symmetry / topology
label.

### 1.3 Equations of motion / dynamics

In the structural regime there is no intrinsic dynamics: it is *static*. The
"evolution" is **geometry optimization** — a quasi-Newton or steepest-descent
flow seeking critical points of $E_{\rm BO}$:

$$
\frac{d \mathbf R_I}{d\tau} = -\nabla_{\mathbf R_I} E_{\rm BO}, \qquad
\frac{d \mathbf h}{d\tau} = -\Omega\,\boldsymbol\sigma(\mathbf h, \mathbf
R)\,\mathbf h^{-\top}
$$

with $\tau$ a fictitious (non-physical) time, used only to reach
$\nabla E_{\rm BO} = 0$. Real time evolution of $(\mathbf R, \mathbf h)$
belongs to the mechanical (cell), thermal (vibrational) or transport
(diffusive) regimes; the structural regime *defines* the manifold of
equilibrium configurations on which those dynamics live.

### 1.4 Conservation laws / constraints

- **Translational invariance**: $E_{\rm BO}$ depends only on relative
  positions, not on the centre of mass. Net force on the system is zero.
- **Rotational invariance**: $E_{\rm BO}$ is invariant under rigid rotation
  of $(\mathbf R, \mathbf h)$ together; total torque is zero.
- **Space-group invariance**: at a symmetric configuration, $E_{\rm BO}$ is
  invariant under the crystal's space group $\mathcal G \subset E(3)$;
  Wyckoff site labels are conserved labels.
- **Cell positivity**: $\det \mathbf h > 0$ (orientation), $\mathbf g
  \succ 0$ (positive-definite metric).
- **Particle number**: $N$ is conserved.

### 1.5 Standard observables

All read from state plus the resolved electronic density:

| Observable | Read from | Operation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Lattice parameters $a,b,c,\alpha,\beta,\gamma$ | $\mathbf h$ | $a = |\mathbf a_1|$ etc., $\cos\alpha = \hat{\mathbf a}_2 \cdot \hat{\mathbf a}_3$ |
| Volume $\Omega$ | $\mathbf h$ | $\det \mathbf h$ |
| Bond lengths $d_{IJ}$ | $\mathbf R_I, \mathbf R_J, \mathbf h$ | $\min_{\mathbf m} \|\mathbf R_I - \mathbf R_J - \mathbf h \mathbf m\|$, $\mathbf m \in \mathbb Z^3$ |
| Bond angles, dihedrals | three / four $\mathbf R$'s | inner products and cross products |
| Forces $\mathbf F_I$ | $E_{\rm BO}$ | $-\nabla_{\mathbf R_I} E_{\rm BO}$ (Hellmann-Feynman + Pulay) |
| Stress $\sigma_{\alpha\beta}$ | $E_{\rm BO}$ | $-\frac{1}{\Omega}\frac{\partial E_{\rm BO}}{\partial \epsilon_{\alpha\beta}}$ |
| Space group label | $\mathbf R, \mathbf h$ | symmetry detection (e.g. tolerance-based) |
| Defect descriptors (vacancy, interstitial) | $\mathbf R$ relative to reference $\mathbf R^{\rm ref}$ | combinatorial matching |
| Surface energy $\gamma_{\rm s}$ | $E_{\rm BO}$ of slab vs bulk | $(E_{\rm slab} - n E_{\rm bulk}) / (2A)$ |

### 1.6 Couplings to other regimes

- **Electronic** (out of group): every evaluation of $E_{\rm BO}$ requires
  the electronic ground state. The coupling is *foundational* — structural
  state determines the external potential that fixes the electron problem.
- **Mechanical** (in group): structural state defines the reference
  configuration $\mathbf h_0$ around which strain is expanded.
- **Thermal** (in group): structural state defines the equilibrium about
  which displacements $\mathbf u_I = \mathbf R_I - \mathbf R_I^{(0)}$ are
  defined and force constants are computed.
- **Magnetic** (out of group): spin configuration enters $E_{\rm BO}$ as a
  collinear / non-collinear constraint on the electronic minimisation.
- **Chemical / surface** (out of group): adsorbate positions enter
  $(\mathbf R, \mathbf h)$; reaction pathways are 1-D curves on the BO
  energy surface.

### 1.7 Numerical methods commonly used

- **Plane-wave** expansion of $n(\mathbf r)$ on a reciprocal-lattice grid
  with energy cutoff (well-suited to periodicity).
- **Atom-centred (Gaussian / NAO)** orbitals; introduce Pulay forces.
- **PAW / pseudopotentials** to separate core from valence electrons.
- Geometry optimisation: BFGS, FIRE, conjugate gradient.
- Symmetry detection: tolerance-based Niggli reduction; Spglib-style
  algorithms.

## Regime 2 — Mechanical

The regime of *how the structure deforms*: stiffness, stress-strain
relations, large-deformation elasticity, hardness trends. Built on top of the
structural regime by considering small or finite perturbations of
$(\mathbf R, \mathbf h)$ about a reference $(\mathbf R^{(0)}, \mathbf
h^{(0)})$.

### 2.1 Mathematical formalism

Define the deformation gradient $\mathbf F = \mathbf h \mathbf h^{(0)\,-1}$
and the **Green-Lagrange (finite) strain** tensor

$$
\boldsymbol\eta = \tfrac12 (\mathbf F^\top \mathbf F - \mathbf I)
= \tfrac12 (\mathbf C - \mathbf I), \qquad \mathbf C = \mathbf F^\top
\mathbf F.
$$

In the infinitesimal limit $\mathbf F = \mathbf I + \nabla \mathbf u$ this
reduces to $\boldsymbol\epsilon = \tfrac12(\nabla \mathbf u + \nabla \mathbf
u^\top)$.

Per-unit-reference-volume **strain energy density** has a Taylor expansion

$$
\Phi(\boldsymbol\eta) = \Phi_0 + \sigma^{(0)}_{ij}\eta_{ij}
+ \tfrac12 C_{ijkl}\,\eta_{ij}\eta_{kl}
+ \tfrac1{3!} C_{ijklmn}\,\eta_{ij}\eta_{kl}\eta_{mn} + \cdots
$$

where $C_{ijkl}$ is the **second-order elastic stiffness tensor** (a 4th-rank
tensor with major and minor symmetries
$C_{ijkl}=C_{jikl}=C_{ijlk}=C_{klij}$, leaving up to 21 independent components
in general anisotropic media, fewer for higher symmetry). In Voigt notation
with index map $11\!\to\!1, 22\!\to\!2, 33\!\to\!3, 23\!\to\!4, 13\!\to\!5,
12\!\to\!6$, $C_{ijkl} \mapsto C_{IJ}$ is a $6\times 6$ symmetric matrix.

At the atomistic level, the strain energy per unit reference volume is
related to the BO total energy through

$$
\Phi(\boldsymbol\eta) = \frac{E_{\rm BO}(\mathbf h(\boldsymbol\eta),
\mathbf R(\boldsymbol\eta)) - E_{\rm BO}^{(0)}}{\Omega_0},
$$

with the internal coordinates $\{\mathbf R_I(\boldsymbol\eta)\}$ relaxed at
each fixed cell to remove internal-stress contamination.

For continuum dynamics, the canonical equation is **Cauchy's first law of
motion**:

$$
\rho \, \frac{\partial^2 u_i}{\partial t^2} = \partial_j \sigma_{ij} + f_i^{\rm
ext}, \qquad \sigma_{ij} = \sigma_{ij}(\boldsymbol\epsilon)
\;\;\text{(constitutive)}.
$$

In Lagrangian (reference) form with the **first Piola-Kirchhoff** stress
$\mathbf P = \mathbf F \mathbf S$, where $\mathbf S$ is the symmetric
**second Piola-Kirchhoff** stress $S_{IJ} = \partial \Phi / \partial
\eta_{IJ}$,

$$
\rho_0 \,\frac{\partial^2 u_I}{\partial t^2} = \nabla_J P_{IJ}
+ \rho_0 f_I^{\rm ext}.
$$

### 2.2 State representation at instant $t$

The mechanical regime can be posed at two scales:

**Atomistic.** State is again $(\mathbf R_I, \mathbf h)$, but the regime
*reads* it as a deformation about a reference $(\mathbf R^{(0)}, \mathbf
h^{(0)})$. No new DOFs are introduced; the regime is a *projection* of the
structural DOFs through the strain map.

**Continuum.** State is the displacement field $\mathbf u(\mathbf X, t):
\Omega_0 \to \mathbb R^3$ on the reference body $\Omega_0$, and, for
dynamics, $\dot{\mathbf u}(\mathbf X, t)$. After spatial discretization on a
mesh with $M$ nodes, the state is $\{\mathbf u_n, \dot{\mathbf u}_n\}_{n=1}^M
\in \mathbb R^{6M}$.

**Constitutive parameters** ($C_{ijkl}$, hardness $H$, yield surface
parameters, etc.) are *not* state — they are functionals of the structural /
electronic state and the temperature.

What is **state**: at the crystal level the same $(\mathbf R, \mathbf h,
\mathbf P, \boldsymbol\Pi_{\mathbf h})$ as the structural / Parrinello-Rahman
phase space, restricted to small / finite excursions from $(\mathbf R^{(0)},
\mathbf h^{(0)})$. At the continuum level, $(\mathbf u, \dot{\mathbf u})$.

What is **derived**: $\boldsymbol\eta$, $\sigma$, $\mathbf P$, $\mathbf S$,
elastic constants $C_{IJ}$, moduli $K, G, E, \nu$, sound velocities.

### 2.3 Equations of motion / dynamics

Three nested cases:

1. **Static equilibrium**: $\nabla_J P_{IJ} + \rho_0 f_I^{\rm ext} = 0$ — an
   elliptic PDE.
2. **Linear elastodynamics**: $\rho \ddot u_i = C_{ijkl} \partial_j \partial_l
   u_k$ — a hyperbolic PDE; the characteristic speeds are the sound
   velocities $v = \sqrt{C/\rho}$.
3. **Finite-strain / plastic / hardness regimes**: nonlinear hyperbolic with
   path-dependent constitutive law (e.g. $\dot{\mathbf F}^p = \dot\gamma
   \,\mathbf s \otimes \mathbf n$ for crystal plasticity).

All three are *Hamiltonian* (or, with dissipation added, Hamiltonian +
Rayleigh dissipation), with conjugate variables $(\mathbf u, \rho \dot{\mathbf
u})$.

### 2.4 Conservation laws / constraints

- **Linear momentum**: total $\int \rho \dot{\mathbf u}\, dV$ conserved if
  external force vanishes.
- **Angular momentum**: conserved if Cauchy stress is symmetric and no body
  torque (which follows from local angular momentum balance).
- **Energy** (in elastic regime): total mechanical energy $\int \tfrac12 \rho
  |\dot{\mathbf u}|^2 + \Phi\, dV$ conserved.
- **Mechanical stability constraints**: $C_{IJ}$ must be positive-definite
  (Born stability) — eigenvalues of the $6\times 6$ Voigt matrix all
  positive. For a cubic crystal this reduces to $C_{11} > |C_{12}|$,
  $C_{11} + 2C_{12} > 0$, $C_{44} > 0$.
- **Symmetry constraints**: the elastic tensor must transform as a 4th-rank
  symmetric tensor under the crystal point group, which sets exact zeros
  and equalities among $C_{IJ}$ (e.g. cubic has only 3 independent
  constants).
- **Cauchy symmetry** ($\sigma_{ij} = \sigma_{ji}$): equivalent to angular
  momentum balance.

### 2.5 Standard observables

| Observable | Read from | Operation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Elastic stiffness $C_{IJ}$ | $\Phi$ | $\partial^2 \Phi / \partial \eta_I \partial \eta_J$ |
| Stress $\sigma_{ij}$ (Cauchy) | $\Phi$ | $J^{-1}\,F_{iK} (\partial \Phi/\partial \eta_{KL}) F_{jL}$ |
| Bulk modulus $K$ | $C_{IJ}$ | $\tfrac19 \sum_{I,J=1}^3 C_{IJ}$ (Voigt) or Reuss / VRH average |
| Shear modulus $G$ | $C_{IJ}$ | Voigt / Reuss / Hill average |
| Young's modulus $E$, Poisson $\nu$ | $K, G$ | $E = 9KG/(3K+G)$, $\nu = (3K-2G)/(2(3K+G))$ |
| Sound velocity $v_\alpha$ | $C_{IJ}, \rho$ | $\rho v^2 = $ eigenvalue of Christoffel matrix $\Gamma_{ik}(\hat{\mathbf q}) = C_{ijkl}\hat q_j \hat q_l$ |
| Yield surface, hardness trend | path-dependent integral of $\sigma:\boldsymbol\epsilon^p$ | constitutive integration |

### 2.6 Couplings to other regimes

- **Structural** (in group): reference configuration; the strain map is
  defined relative to it.
- **Thermal** (in group): elastic constants are temperature dependent; via
  the QHA, $C_{IJ}(T) = \partial^2 F(T, \boldsymbol\eta) / \partial \eta_I
  \partial \eta_J$. The acoustic phonon branches at $\mathbf q \to 0$
  determine the sound velocities, giving a *direct* identity between the
  $\mathbf q \to 0$ limit of the dynamical matrix and the elastic tensor:

  $$
  \sum_{\beta\delta} C_{\alpha\beta\gamma\delta} \hat q_\beta \hat
  q_\delta = \rho \, v_{\alpha\gamma}^2(\hat{\mathbf q}).
  $$

- **Electronic** (out of group): $C_{IJ}$ is the second strain derivative of
  the BO energy; piezoelectricity couples strain to electric polarisation
  $\mathbf P_{\rm el} = e_{i\,IJ}\eta_{IJ}$.
- **Magnetic** (out of group): magnetostriction; magnetic anisotropy energy
  couples strain to magnetisation direction.
- **Transport** (out of group): mobility / migration barriers shift under
  strain (deformation potentials).

### 2.7 Numerical methods commonly used

- Stress-strain by finite differences (apply a strain pattern, relax
  internal $\mathbf R$, compute $\sigma$).
- Density-functional perturbation theory (DFPT) for the second derivative
  $\partial^2 E / \partial \eta_I \partial \eta_J$ directly.
- For continuum: finite elements (P1, P2), spectral / Fourier methods on
  periodic cells, finite differences with staggered grids.
- Acoustic-tensor (Christoffel) diagonalisation for sound velocities.

## Regime 3 — Thermal

The regime of *small motions* about the structural equilibrium and what they
do collectively: phonon spectra, heat capacity, thermal expansion, thermal
conductivity.

### 3.1 Mathematical formalism

Expand $E_{\rm BO}$ about the equilibrium $\mathbf R^{(0)}$:

$$
E_{\rm BO} \;=\; E_0 + \tfrac12 \sum_{I\alpha, J\beta} \Phi_{I\alpha,J\beta}\,
u_{I\alpha} u_{J\beta}
+ \tfrac1{3!} \sum \Psi_{I\alpha,J\beta,K\gamma}\,u_{I\alpha}u_{J\beta}
u_{K\gamma} + \cdots,
$$

where $\mathbf u_I = \mathbf R_I - \mathbf R_I^{(0)}$, $\Phi_{I\alpha,J\beta}
= \partial^2 E_{\rm BO}/\partial R_{I\alpha} \partial R_{J\beta}|_0$ is the
**second-order force-constant matrix** (the Hessian), and $\Psi$ is the
third-order tensor of anharmonic constants.

The **harmonic Hamiltonian** is

$$
\hat H_{\rm harm} = \sum_{I\alpha} \frac{\hat P_{I\alpha}^2}{2 M_I}
+ \tfrac12 \sum_{I\alpha, J\beta} \Phi_{I\alpha,J\beta}\,\hat u_{I\alpha}\hat
u_{J\beta}.
$$

For a periodic crystal, Bloch-decompose $\hat u_{I\alpha}(\mathbf R) =
\sum_{\mathbf q} \hat u_{I\alpha}(\mathbf q)\,e^{i\mathbf q \cdot \mathbf R}$
and define the **dynamical matrix**

$$
D_{I\alpha, J\beta}(\mathbf q) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{M_I M_J}}
\sum_{\mathbf R} \Phi_{I\alpha, J\beta}(\mathbf R)\, e^{-i\mathbf q \cdot
\mathbf R}.
$$

Diagonalising $D(\mathbf q)$ yields branches $s$ with eigenvalues
$\omega_{\mathbf q s}^2$ and polarisation vectors $\boldsymbol\xi_{\mathbf q
s, I}$. The Hamiltonian becomes diagonal in second quantization:

$$
\hat H_{\rm harm} = \sum_{\mathbf q, s} \hbar \omega_{\mathbf q s} \big(\hat
a^\dagger_{\mathbf q s} \hat a_{\mathbf q s} + \tfrac12\big),
\qquad [\hat a_{\mathbf q s}, \hat a_{\mathbf q' s'}^\dagger] = \delta_{\mathbf
q \mathbf q'} \delta_{s s'}.
$$

Beyond harmonic: the **quasi-harmonic Helmholtz free energy** is

$$
F(T, \boldsymbol\eta) = E_{\rm BO}(\boldsymbol\eta) + \tfrac12
\sum_{\mathbf q s} \hbar \omega_{\mathbf q s}(\boldsymbol\eta)
+ k_B T \sum_{\mathbf q s} \ln\!\Big[ 1 - e^{-\hbar
\omega_{\mathbf q s}(\boldsymbol\eta)/k_B T}\Big],
$$

with the $\boldsymbol\eta$-dependence of frequencies captured by **mode
Grüneisen parameters** $\gamma_{\mathbf q s} = -\partial \ln \omega_{\mathbf q
s}/\partial \ln \Omega$.

Far from harmonic: **phonon Boltzmann transport equation** for the
distribution $n_{\mathbf q s}(\mathbf r, t)$:

$$
\frac{\partial n_{\mathbf q s}}{\partial t} + \mathbf v_{\mathbf q s} \cdot
\nabla_{\mathbf r} n_{\mathbf q s} = \mathcal C[n],
$$

with $\mathbf v_{\mathbf q s} = \nabla_{\mathbf q} \omega_{\mathbf q s}$ and
the collision integral $\mathcal C[n]$ given by three-phonon (and
four-phonon, isotope, defect) scattering rates built from $\Psi$ via Fermi's
golden rule. In the relaxation-time approximation $\mathcal C \approx
(n^{(0)} - n)/\tau_{\mathbf q s}$.

### 3.2 State representation at instant $t$

Three reasonable choices, depending on the level of description:

(a) **Classical microscopic** (molecular dynamics regime): state is again
$(\mathbf R_I, \mathbf P_I)$, $\dim = 6N$, with the thermal regime
*reading* it through the lens of small displacements about $\mathbf
R^{(0)}$.

(b) **Quantum / second-quantized**: state is the set of mode occupation
numbers $\{n_{\mathbf q s}\}$ for each branch and wavevector on the
Born-von Kármán grid. With $N_c$ unit cells and $r$ atoms per cell, there
are $3rN_c$ modes; the state at thermal equilibrium is the Bose-Einstein
distribution
$n_{\mathbf q s}^{(0)} = (\exp(\hbar \omega_{\mathbf q s}/k_BT) - 1)^{-1}$
and is fully specified by the single scalar $T$.

(c) **Semiclassical transport**: state is the distribution function
$n_{\mathbf q s}(\mathbf r, t)$ — a function on phase space.

What is **state**, irreducibly: in case (a) the same atomic phase-space DOFs;
in case (b) the occupation numbers; in case (c) the phase-space distribution.

What is **derived**: $\omega_{\mathbf q s}$ (eigenvalues of $D(\mathbf q)$,
which itself is a *parameter* derived from structural state and BO
Hessian), heat capacity $C_v(T)$, density of states $g(\omega)$, thermal
conductivity tensor $\kappa_{ij}$.

### 3.3 Equations of motion / dynamics

- **Classical MD**: $\dot{\mathbf R}_I = \mathbf P_I/M_I$, $\dot{\mathbf P}_I
  = -\nabla_{\mathbf R_I} E_{\rm BO}$. Newton's equations; integrated by a
  symplectic scheme (velocity-Verlet) to preserve phase-space volume.
- **Harmonic quantum**: $i\hbar \,d\hat a_{\mathbf q s}/dt = [\hat a_{\mathbf
  q s}, \hat H] = \hbar \omega_{\mathbf q s} \hat a_{\mathbf q s}$ —
  uncoupled harmonic-oscillator evolution.
- **Boltzmann transport**: the BTE above, an integro-PDE on phase space.
- **QHA "evolution"** (in $T$): the equilibrium $(\mathbf h(T), \mathbf
  R(T))$ moves with temperature by $\partial G / \partial \boldsymbol\eta =
  0$ at each $T$.

### 3.4 Conservation laws / constraints

- **Total energy** (microcanonical MD); the **harmonic invariants**
  $n_{\mathbf q s}$ separately conserved in the strict harmonic limit.
- **Crystal momentum** $\hbar \mathbf q$: conserved modulo reciprocal-lattice
  vectors (Umklapp processes change it by $\mathbf G$).
- **Acoustic sum rule**: translational invariance of $E_{\rm BO}$ forces
  $\sum_J \Phi_{I\alpha, J\beta} = 0$ for each $(I,\alpha,\beta)$.
  Equivalently $D(\mathbf q\!\to\!0)$ has three zero eigenvalues —
  the acoustic branches start at zero frequency.
- **Hermiticity / positivity**: $D(\mathbf q)$ Hermitian; eigenvalues
  $\omega^2 \ge 0$ at every $\mathbf q$ in a mechanically stable phase
  (imaginary modes signal a structural instability).
- **Rotational sum rules**: rotational invariance constrains higher
  derivatives of $E_{\rm BO}$.
- **Bose-Einstein constraint** at equilibrium: occupations $\ge 0$.
- **Particle-number conservation in BTE collisions**: three-phonon
  scattering changes $\{n_{\mathbf q s}\}$ while conserving energy and
  crystal momentum (modulo $\mathbf G$).

### 3.5 Standard observables

| Observable | Read from | Operation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Phonon dispersion $\omega_{\mathbf q s}$ | $D(\mathbf q)$ | eigenvalues |
| Density of states $g(\omega)$ | $\omega_{\mathbf q s}$ | $g(\omega) = \sum_{\mathbf q s} \delta(\omega - \omega_{\mathbf q s})$ |
| Heat capacity $C_v(T)$ | $g(\omega)$ | $C_v = k_B \int (\hbar\omega/k_B T)^2 \frac{e^{\hbar\omega/k_BT}}{(e^{\hbar\omega/k_BT}-1)^2} g(\omega)\,d\omega$ |
| Mode Grüneisen $\gamma_{\mathbf q s}$ | $\omega_{\mathbf q s}(\Omega)$ | $-d\ln\omega/d\ln\Omega$ |
| Thermal expansion $\alpha$ | $F(T,\boldsymbol\eta)$ | $\alpha_{IJ} = \frac{1}{V} S_{IJKL} \sum_{\mathbf q s} \gamma^{(KL)}_{\mathbf q s}\, c_v(\omega_{\mathbf q s})$, with $S = C^{-1}$ the elastic **compliance** and $\gamma^{(KL)}_{\mathbf q s} = -\partial \ln \omega_{\mathbf q s}/\partial \eta_{KL}$ the strain-Grüneisen tensor (the scalar $\gamma_{\mathbf q s}$ above is its volumetric trace) |
| Thermal conductivity $\kappa_{ij}$ | $n_{\mathbf q s}, \mathbf v_{\mathbf q s}, \tau_{\mathbf q s}$ | $\kappa_{ij} = \sum_{\mathbf q s} c_{\mathbf q s} v_i v_j \tau_{\mathbf q s}$ |
| Mean square displacement $\langle u_{I\alpha}^2\rangle$ | $\omega_{\mathbf q s}, \boldsymbol\xi$ | $\sum_{\mathbf q s} \frac{\hbar}{2 M_I \omega_{\mathbf q s}}\coth(\hbar\omega/2k_BT)\,|\xi_{\mathbf q s, I\alpha}|^2$ |
| Debye-Waller factor | $\langle u^2\rangle$ | $W = \tfrac12 \langle (\mathbf q \cdot \mathbf u)^2\rangle$ |

### 3.6 Couplings to other regimes

- **Structural** (in group): equilibrium configuration is the expansion
  point; the dynamical matrix is the Hessian of $E_{\rm BO}$.
- **Mechanical** (in group): the $\mathbf q \to 0$ acoustic-branch sound
  velocities equal the elastic-tensor sound velocities (long-wavelength
  identity). Elastic constants are $T$-dependent via the QHA.
- **Electronic** (out of group): **electron-phonon coupling**
  $g_{mn}^{\nu}(\mathbf k, \mathbf q) = \langle \psi_{m,\mathbf k+\mathbf q}
  | \partial_{\mathbf q \nu} V | \psi_{n,\mathbf k}\rangle / \sqrt{2 M
  \omega_{\mathbf q \nu}}$ (single-species shorthand; in a multi-species cell
  the $1/\sqrt{2M\omega}$ factor generalizes to per-atom masses $M_\kappa$
  carried by the mass-weighted phonon-eigenvector normalization
  $\sum_\kappa M_\kappa |\mathbf e_\kappa|^2 = 1$) — drives phonon-limited resistivity,
  superconductivity, Allen-Heine-Cardona temperature renormalisation of
  band gaps (Fan + Debye-Waller terms), polaron formation.
- **Optical** (out of group): IR absorption couples photons to
  zone-centre polar (LO/TO-split) optical phonons via Born effective
  charges $Z^*_{I,\alpha\beta} = \Omega \partial P_\alpha / \partial
  u_{I\beta}$.
- **Magnetic** (out of group): spin-lattice coupling; magnetic exchange
  constants $J(\mathbf R)$ depend on $\mathbf R$; magnons hybridise with
  acoustic phonons.
- **Transport** (out of group): phonon-drag thermoelectric coefficient;
  carrier mobility set by electron-phonon scattering.
- **Thermodynamic** (out of group): the QHA $F(T, \boldsymbol\eta)$ is the
  workhorse for finite-$T$ phase stability and free-energy curves.
- **Chemical / surface** (out of group): zero-point + thermal corrections
  to adsorption energies and reaction barriers.

### 3.7 Numerical methods commonly used

- **Frozen phonon / finite-displacement**: displace atoms by small $\Delta$,
  compute forces, reconstruct $\Phi$ in a supercell consistent with chosen
  $\mathbf q$-mesh.
- **DFPT** for $\Phi$ at arbitrary $\mathbf q$ from primitive-cell
  perturbations.
- Fourier interpolation of $\Phi(\mathbf R)$ to dense $\mathbf q$-grids;
  acoustic sum rule restored numerically by symmetrization.
- Boltzmann transport solvers (ShengBTE, AlmaBTE, Phono3py, ALAMODE): direct
  iterative solution or RTA, with three-phonon (and increasingly four-phonon)
  scattering kernels built from $\Psi$.
- Velocity-Verlet, Nosé-Hoover, Langevin thermostats for finite-$T$ MD.

## Cross-regime synthesis

### S.1 What mathematical structure is shared?

All three regimes are **variational over the same configuration manifold**

$$
\mathcal Q = \{(\mathbf R_I, \mathbf h)\} \subset \mathbb R^{3N} \times
\mathrm{GL}^+(3),
$$

possibly enlarged to the phase space $T^*\mathcal Q$ when conjugate momenta
are included. They differ only in what *functional* on $\mathcal Q$ they
foreground:

| Regime | Functional foregrounded | Derivative order it cares about |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Structural | $E_{\rm BO}(\mathbf R, \mathbf h)$ | 0 (the value) and 1 (forces, stress) |
| Mechanical | $E_{\rm BO}$ restricted to a strain map; or $F(T, \boldsymbol\eta)$ | 2 in $\boldsymbol\eta$ (elastic constants) |
| Thermal | $E_{\rm BO}$ Hessian in atomic displacements; $F(T, \boldsymbol\eta)$ in $\boldsymbol\eta$ | 2 in $\mathbf u$ (dynamical matrix), 3+ in $\mathbf u$ (anharmonic), 1 in $T$ (entropy) |

In Hamiltonian form, all three are sub-systems of one master Hamiltonian

$$
H(\mathbf R, \mathbf P, \mathbf h, \boldsymbol\Pi_{\mathbf h}; T)
= \sum_I \frac{\mathbf P_I^2}{2 M_I}
+ \frac{\operatorname{tr}(\boldsymbol\Pi_{\mathbf h}^\top \boldsymbol\Pi_{\mathbf h})}{2 W}
+ E_{\rm BO}(\mathbf R, \mathbf h) \;\;[+ \text{thermostat / cell terms}]
$$

(the Parrinello-Rahman extended Hamiltonian). Each regime is a projection:
structural = zero-momentum, zero-temperature stationary points; mechanical =
quadratic expansion in $\boldsymbol\eta$; thermal = quadratic/cubic expansion
in $\mathbf u$ plus statistical averaging at temperature $T$.

The conservation structure is correspondingly shared: translational and
rotational invariance of $E_{\rm BO}$ produces **all three regime-specific
sum rules**:

- structural: zero net force and torque,
- mechanical: zero-modes of the elastic tensor (rigid translations /
  rotations are not strain),
- thermal: acoustic sum rule (three zero modes of $D(\mathbf q\!\to\!0)$,
  giving three acoustic branches).

These are *one symmetry, three faces*.

### S.2 What is the deepest common canonical?

The single object from which all three regimes can be derived is the
**Born-Oppenheimer total-energy functional** $E_{\rm BO}(\mathbf R, \mathbf
h)$, together with its **partition function**

$$
Z(T, \mathbf h) = \int e^{-\beta E_{\rm BO}(\mathbf R, \mathbf h) - \beta \sum
\mathbf P_I^2/(2M_I)}\, d\mathbf R\,d\mathbf P,
$$

(or its quantum-mechanical variant) and the resulting Helmholtz free energy
$F(T, \mathbf h) = -k_B T \ln Z$. From these two:

- Structural observables = critical points and low derivatives of $E_{\rm
  BO}$ (at $T=0$) or of $F$ (at $T>0$).
- Mechanical observables = second strain-derivatives of $F$ (with internal
  $\mathbf R$ relaxed): $C_{IJ}(T) = (1/\Omega) \partial^2 F /
  \partial \eta_I \partial \eta_J$.
- Thermal observables = displacement-Hessian eigenvalues of $E_{\rm BO}$
  (harmonic phonons), volume-derivatives of $\omega$ (Grüneisen), and
  the kinetic / collision pieces extracted from time-correlation
  functions of $\hat H$.

A single hierarchy:

```
E_{BO}(R, h)
   |
   |--- min over R, h:   STRUCTURAL ground state
   |
   |--- Hessian in R at fixed h:   D(q), phonons          --- THERMAL
   |       |
   |       |--- Bose-Einstein average:  F_vib(T, h)
   |       |
   |       |--- + E_BO:  F(T, h)
   |               |
   |               |--- Hessian in h (strain):  C_{IJ}(T)  --- MECHANICAL
   |               |--- min in h at each T:   alpha(T)    --- THERMAL/MECH
   |               |
   |--- Hessian in h directly at T=0:  C_{IJ}(0)            --- MECHANICAL
   |
   |--- derivative chain w.r.t. external fields:  Z*, dielectric, ...
            (couples out of group)
```

### S.3 What is irreducibly different?

Three things resist unification:

1. **The role of time / statistics.** Structural is *static* (critical points,
   $T=0$ in the strict sense). Mechanical is *deterministic dynamics on a
   continuum field* (hyperbolic PDE for elastic waves; nonlinear
   path-dependent for plasticity). Thermal mixes *quantum statistical*
   averaging (Bose-Einstein) with *semiclassical transport* (Boltzmann
   equation on phase space). These are three distinct mathematical
   categories: variational geometry, hyperbolic PDE dynamics, and kinetic
   theory.

2. **The relevant DOF dimension.** Structural minimally needs $3N + 6$ DOFs.
   Mechanical (continuum) needs a *field* — the displacement $\mathbf u(\mathbf
   X)$ on $\Omega_0$, infinite-dimensional. Thermal (BTE) needs a *distribution
   on phase space* $n_{\mathbf q s}(\mathbf r, t)$, also infinite-dimensional
   but on a different phase space (reciprocal space + position). They embed
   into a common atomistic configuration but emerge as different limits with
   different effective state spaces.

3. **The privileged dependent variable.** Structural foregrounds the
   *configuration* itself. Mechanical foregrounds the *strain* (a derivative
   of configuration). Thermal foregrounds the *spectrum* (eigenvalues of a
   second derivative). The maps from state to observable are derivatives of
   different orders.

### S.4 What this means for a unified state representation

A consistent atomistic state vector for all three regimes is

$$
\boxed{\;
s(t) = \big(\, \mathbf h(t),\; \{\mathbf R_I(t)\}_{I=1}^N,\; \{\mathbf
P_I(t)\}_{I=1}^N,\; \boldsymbol\Pi_{\mathbf h}(t),\; \{Z_I\}\,\big)
\;}
$$

with $\dim s = 9 + 6N + 9 + N_{\rm species}$, restricted to $\mathbf h \in
\mathrm{GL}^+(3)$. Every observable above (structural lattice parameters,
elastic constants, phonon frequencies, heat capacity, thermal conductivity)
is computable from $s$ together with the BO energy functional. Continuum
fields ($\mathbf u(\mathbf X)$) and reciprocal-space distributions
($n_{\mathbf q s}$) are **emergent representations** obtained by coarse-graining
or Bloch-transforming $s$ — not part of the irreducible state.

A residual / constraint catalogue for PINO use ranges over

- structural: $\nabla_{\mathbf R} E_{\rm BO} = 0$, $\partial E_{\rm BO}/\partial
  \mathbf h = -\Omega \boldsymbol\sigma$ matches measured stress;
- mechanical: $C_{IJ}$ positive-definite; Cauchy momentum balance; sound
  velocity = $\mathbf q\to 0$ phonon limit;
- thermal: acoustic sum rule on $\Phi$ (translational invariance); Hermiticity
  and non-negativity of $D(\mathbf q)$; energy / momentum conservation in MD;
  detailed balance in the BTE collision kernel.

All three regimes ultimately reduce to **identities on derivatives of
$E_{\rm BO}$** — a single functional whose value, gradients, and Hessians
the operator must respect.
